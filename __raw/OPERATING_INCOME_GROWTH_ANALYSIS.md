**Continental: 46.8% â†’ -145.7% (-192.5pt swing)**

4. **Pricing pressure**
   - Had to match LCC prices to fill planes
   - Revenue per seat fell
   - **Race to the bottom on fares**

## **Q&A:**

### **Q: Why is WestJet's -2.3% so much better than everyone else?**

**A:** Three factors:

**1. Low Fixed Costs:**
- Can adjust capacity fast (no hub constraints)
- Minimal legacy costs (pensions, old IT)
- Union flexibility (or non-union)

**2. Operating Margin Buffer:**
- Started with 13.9% margin (high)
- Dropped to 11.5% (still profitable)
- Competitors had 4-8% margins â†’ went negative

**3. Cost Discipline:**
- Operating expenses rose only 3.1pts
- Competitors saw 5-10pt increases
- Every dollar of revenue still profitable

### **Q: How did Continental grow revenue (+7.1%) but collapse profit (-145.7%)?**

**A:** The death spiral:

**Revenue:**
- $14.2B â†’ $15.2B (+$1.0B, +7.1%)
- Had to keep flying to cover fixed costs
- Slashed prices to fill planes

**Costs:**
- Operating expenses: $13.1B â†’ $15.1B (+$2.0B, +15.7%)
- Fuel spiked (no hedging)
- Fixed costs couldn't be cut
- **Costs rose 2x faster than revenue**

**Result:**
- Operating income: $687M â†’ -$314M
- Lost $1.0B in profit while gaining $1.0B in revenue
- **Every new revenue dollar cost $2 to generate**

### **Q: Why did Singapore go from best (61.7%) to negative (-57.5%)?**

**A:** Premium vulnerability:

**2007 (61.7%):**
- Strong business class demand
- Asian expansion
- Premium pricing power

**2008 (-57.5%):**
- Business travel froze (corporate cost cuts)
- Premium cabins empty (70% of revenue, 30% of seats)
- Long-haul routes unprofitable
- **Premium positioning = fragile in recession**

**Contrast with WestJet:**
- No business class to lose
- Economy demand more stable (people still fly, just cheaper)
- **Downside protection from low-price positioning**
